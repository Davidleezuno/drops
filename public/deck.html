Q&A appendix

HitPay store builder / payment links

“Isn’t this just a thin wrapper on what HitPay already has?”

A store builder assumes a permanent catalogue and a business willing to configure it. Drops is a different object: a batch that is gone tonight, sold by a person who would never set up a store.

It is an on-ramp, not a competitor. These sellers use PayNow screenshots today, completely off HitPay’s rails. Drops pulls every sale into HitPay payment requests.

If pushed“The store builder fixes the shopfront. It leaves manual order capture and screenshot reconciliation—which is most of her actual pain.”

Q1 / 7

Business model

Volume + acquisition

“How does this make money?”

It rides the same engine as HitPay: payment volume. Every drop is born as HitPay payment requests, driving GMV and take-rate from micro-sellers who transact off-platform today.

Standalone, the natural extension is a small SaaS tier for weekly power sellers. For HitPay, the immediate value is a new acquisition hook plus repeat volume.

Keep the answer honestDo not invent a fee model on the spot. Frame it as the volume-and-acquisition wedge a payments company actually values.

Q2 / 7

The last-unit race

Why PAID_LATE exists

“A customer paid and got nothing. Why not reserve stock?”

We built and rejected TTL holds. During a 90-second rush, an abandoned five-minute hold can block inventory from a real paying buyer and cause underselling.

Instead, only a validated paid webhook decrements stock in one guarded atomic write. Exactly one buyer wins the last unit; rare stragglers are flagged and refunded through HitPay.

Proof, not theory“race:proof runs 12 concurrent real sandbox payments: exactly 4 PAID, 8 PAID_LATE, and stock never exceeds 4.”

Q3 / 7

Seller adoption

Do not change the workflow—delete work from it.

“WhatsApp already works. Will an auntie switch?”

She uploads the exact photo she already sends. There is no catalogue project, no settings maze, and no new distribution channel to learn.

The output is one link for the same TikTok bio and WhatsApp status she already uses. Buyers need no app and no account.

The switching-cost line“We are not changing how she finds customers. We are deleting the ten messages and screenshot matching after they arrive.”

Q4 / 7

The 3D storefront

Charm is not the moat.

“Isn’t the 3D shop a gimmick anyone can copy?”

The 3D gives a one-person seller identity and a live social feeling that a checkout form cannot. It earns attention, but it is not the defensibility claim.

The wedge is off-platform reach plus 60-second setup plus self-reconciling payments. The roadmap compounds that with returning-buyer memory and split settlement.

Say it plainly“The moat is not the polygons. It is turning any audience, on any channel, into reconciled commerce.”

Q6 / 7

Scope

The wedge is not the ceiling.

“Only drops? Sellers also want an always-on store.”

Always-on is the same object with the window turned off. We lead with the drop because it is the seller’s moment of highest pain, urgency and conversion.

That sharp use case earns adoption. The same catalogue, buyer identity, fulfilment and payment rails can expand into ongoing commerce later.

The scope line“Drops is the wedge, not the ceiling. We start where existing store builders are weakest.”
